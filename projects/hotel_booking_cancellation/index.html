Predicting Hotel Booking Cancellations with Machine Learning

Chad Vo, Kristen Lin, Emily Zhao, Karan Patel

•

Tech Stack: Python, TensorFlow, Scikit-learn, Neural Networks, XGBoost, Random Forest

Summary: This project explores how machine learning can be used to predict hotel reservation cancellations by modeling customer booking behavior, timing, and reservation characteristics using tree-based models and neural networks.

TL;DR: Hotel cancellations create revenue loss and operational inefficiency. By leveraging XGBoost on booking data, we achieved a model with 86% precision and an ROC AUC of 0.93, outperforming Logistic Regression and Neural Networks in identifying high-risk reservations.

1. Introduction: The Cost of Uncertainty

Hotel booking cancellations are a major challenge in the hospitality industry, leading to direct revenue loss and difficulties in resource allocation. While some cancellations are driven by impulse, others follow predictable patterns based on booking lead times, deposit types, and market segments.

The goal of this project was to move from reactive cancellation management to proactive decision-making. [cite_start]By developing machine learning models capable of analyzing large-scale reservation data, we aimed to identify high-risk bookings before they cancel, allowing hotels to implement dynamic pricing or personalized retention strategies[cite: 17, 19, 21].

2. The Dataset & Engineering

[cite_start]We utilized the Hotel Booking Demand dataset from Kaggle, containing 119,390 reservations from a Portuguese hotel chain (2015–2017)[cite: 36]. [cite_start]The dataset included 32 features covering booking timing, demographics, and special requests[cite: 37].

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Before modeling, we performed extensive EDA to understand the underlying patterns of cancellations. [cite_start]We observed stable cancellation rates throughout the year, fluctuating between 30% and 40%, with slight dips during the start and end of the year[cite: 83].

We also analyzed feature correlations to identify which factors most strongly influenced cancellation likelihood. [cite_start]The heatmap data revealed distinct drivers for cancellations versus kept bookings[cite: 85].

3. Methodology & Models

[cite_start]We approached this as a binary classification problem, comparing four distinct algorithms to balance precision and recall[cite: 23].

The Models:

    [cite_start]
  • Logistic Regression (Baseline): Provided a baseline accuracy of ~81% but struggled with non-linear relationships, resulting in low recall (60%)[cite: 122, 123].
    • [cite_start]
  • Random Forest: An ensemble method that improved performance by capturing non-linear interactions through majority voting, reducing the risk of overfitting[cite: 127, 129].
  • XGBoost: A gradient boosting framework that builds trees sequentially to correct previous errors. [cite_start]We tuned it with 200 trees and a max depth of 15, handling the dataset's complexity and class imbalance effectively[cite: 134, 138, 140].
  • Neural Network: A multi-layer perceptron with ReLU activation and dropout regularization. [cite_start]While powerful, it was computationally intensive and slightly less robust than the tree-based models for this specific structured data[cite: 145, 146, 149].

Key Findings:

    [cite_start]
  • XGBoost was the superior model: It achieved the highest F1-score (0.80) and ROC AUC (0.93), successfully capturing complex patterns that the baseline missed[cite: 192].
  • Feature Importance: As shown in Table 1, the "Deposit Type" (specifically non-refundable) and "Lead Time" were the strongest predictors of cancellation. [cite_start]Guests who made special requests were significantly less likely to cancel[cite: 112].

This study demonstrated that machine learning can effectively distinguish between reliable bookings and likely cancellations. [cite_start]While Logistic Regression provided interpretability, ensemble methods like XGBoost offered the necessary complexity to maximize precision[cite: 190, 192].
